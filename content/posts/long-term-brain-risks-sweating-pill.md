## The pill that "works" — at a cost nobody explains

You walked into the dermatologist's office dripping through your shirt. Fifteen minutes later, you walked out with a prescription for glycopyrrolate or oxybutynin. It worked. Your palms dried up. Your shirts survived the day.

But nobody mentioned what anticholinergics actually do inside your skull.

> "The association between anticholinergic drug use and dementia risk has been demonstrated across multiple large-scale cohort studies." — *JAMA Internal Medicine, 2019*

**Acetylcholine is the neurotransmitter your brain uses for memory formation, attention, and learning.** These medications suppress it system-wide. That's not a side effect — it's the mechanism of action.

## The research nobody's reading

A 2019 study published in *JAMA Internal Medicine* followed over 284,000 patients aged 55 and older. Those with the highest anticholinergic exposure had a **50% increased risk of dementia** compared to non-users.

The kicker: the risk began accumulating at relatively modest doses — doses well within the range prescribed for hyperhidrosis.

This isn't one study. It's a pattern across multiple research groups, multiple countries, and multiple years of follow-up.

## What this means for HH patients

Most people with hyperhidrosis start these medications young — often in their 20s or 30s. They're told it's "safe" and "well-tolerated." And in the short term, that's often true.

But **no one is tracking the cumulative anticholinergic burden** in young HH patients who may take these drugs for decades.

The conversation your doctor isn't having with you: What's the exit strategy? What happens at year 10? Year 20?

## The alternative nobody's offering

This isn't about scaring you off your medication. It's about informed consent — and about asking whether a lifestyle-based approach could reduce your dependence on a drug class that carries real long-term risk.

Stress management, nervous system regulation, nutritional optimization, circadian health — these aren't "alternatives" to medication. They're the foundation that should have come first.

**Your dermatologist gave you 15 minutes and a prescription. You deserve more than that.**
